What We Tested

We spent three months testing 15 budget smartphones under $400, evaluating them across key categories:

  • Performance: Daily usage, gaming, multitasking
  • Camera Quality: Photo and video in various lighting conditions
  • Display: Brightness, color accuracy, refresh rate
  • Battery Life: Real-world usage patterns
  • Build Quality: Materials, durability, design
  • Software: Update frequency, bloatware, user experience

Key Findings

Performance Has Caught Up

Budget phones in 2026 offer performance that was flagship-level just two years ago. The Snapdragon 7+ Gen 3 and Tensor G3 chips deliver smooth performance for 95% of users.

Cameras Are the New Battleground

The biggest improvements are in computational photography. Budget phones now offer Night mode, Portrait mode, and AI-enhanced photos that rival expensive flagships.

Displays Are Finally Premium

OLED displays with 120Hz refresh rates are now standard in the $300+ range. The days of dim, washed-out budget phone screens are over.

Phones to Avoid

Not every budget phone is worth buying. Here are the ones we'd skip:

  • Samsung Galaxy A25: Slow performance, poor camera
  • Motorola Moto G Power: Outdated software, low-resolution display
  • TCL 40 XE: Build quality issues, inconsistent performance

Buying Advice

If you want the best camera: Google Pixel 8a

If you want the best performance: OnePlus Nord 4

If you want the best value: Xiaomi Redmi Note 13 Pro

If you want long-term support: Google Pixel 8a (7 years of updates)

If you want the biggest battery: OnePlus Nord 4 (5500mAh)

The Bottom Line

Budget smartphones in 2026 offer incredible value. You can get flagship-level features for under $400, making expensive phones harder to justify for most users.

Our top recommendation is the Google Pixel 8a for its superior camera, clean software, and long-term support. But the OnePlus Nord 4 and Xiaomi Redmi Note 13 Pro are excellent alternatives depending on your priorities.
